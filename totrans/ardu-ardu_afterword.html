AFTERWORD

At this point, having read about (and I hope built) the 70 projects in this book, you should have a much deeper and broader understanding of electronics and the Arduino environment, along with the knowledge and confidence required to create more complex and useful Arduino-based projects.

You’ve learned to interface with a wide variety of devices outside the Arduino ecosystem via data buses and different forms of wireless communication, including Bluetooth and the Telegram instant-messaging platform. With your newfound skills, you might build devices that control mains outlets via the internet, create web interfaces to monitor sensor data, add MP3-quality audio to your projects to generate user notifications, use watchdog timers to improve project operation reliability, and much more.

I hope this book has inspired you to keep exploring the world of electrical engineering. You won’t be alone in doing so. There’s a thriving community of Arduino users you can find on the internet in places like the following:

The official Arduino forum https://forum.arduino.cc

The hackaday.io website https://hackaday.io/list/3611-arduino-projects

Instructables https://www.instructables.com/circuits/arduino/projects

Reddit https://www.reddit.com/r/arduino

You can also search for the #arduino hashtag on social media for project ideas and inspiration or seek out a local hackerspace or club, either online or in person.

I’m happy to receive feedback about this book via the contact details on the publisher’s web page: https://nostarch.com/contactus. While you’re there, don’t forget to check out my other books published by No Starch Press, including AVR Workshop, which takes you into the world of 8-bit microcontrollers that power the Arduino ecosystem.

As always, don’t just sit there—make something!
